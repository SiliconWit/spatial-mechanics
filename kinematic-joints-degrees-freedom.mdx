<InArticleAd />


<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">

### The Joint Analysis Challenge

Engineers across different domains face critical questions:

:::note[Critical Design Questions]
- **Which joint types** provide required motion capabilities within space constraints?
- **How many actuators** are needed to achieve desired degrees of freedom?
- **What constraints** (closure, coupling, external) reduce effective mobility?
- **How do we balance** complexity, cost, and performance in joint selection?
- **Can underactuation** provide advantages over fully actuated systems?
:::

> **Engineering Question:** How do we systematically analyze joint configurations to select optimal mechanisms for constrained environments, multi-robot coordination, and adaptive grasping tasks?

<details>
<summary>**Click to Reveal: Why DoF Analysis Matters**</summary>
**Consequences of Inadequate Analysis:**
- **Motion limitations** preventing required surgical maneuvers or assembly tasks
- **Over-actuated designs** wasting motors, cost, and control complexity
- **Closure constraints** causing unexpected loss of mobility in multi-robot cells
- **Workspace restrictions** from unaccounted external constraints (trocar ports, mounting)
- **Inefficient grippers** requiring multiple motors when one could suffice

**Benefits of Systematic DoF Analysis:**
- **Optimal actuator count** - knowing exactly how many motors needed
- **Constraint accounting** - predicting effective DoF under real operating conditions
- **Design trade-offs** - comparing alternatives quantitatively (size, force, complexity)
- **Underactuation opportunities** - exploiting passive compliance for adaptability
- **Cost reduction** - minimizing actuators while maintaining functionality
</details>

### Degrees of Freedom Analysis

<Card title="üî¢ Gr√ºbler's Equation for Spatial Mechanisms" icon="document">
$$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

**Where:**
- $F$ = Degrees of freedom of the mechanism
- $n$ = Number of links (including ground)  
- $j$ = Number of joints
- $c_i$ = Number of constraints imposed by joint $i$

**Physical Meaning:** The mobility of a spatial mechanism equals the total possible motion of all links minus the constraints imposed by all joints.
</Card>

:::tip[Understanding Constraints]
A **free body in 3D space** has 6 degrees of freedom:
- **3 translations** (X, Y, Z directions)  
- **3 rotations** (about X, Y, Z axes)

Each joint **removes some of these freedoms** through constraints.
> If the mechanism's <Badge text="unconstrained motion" variant="caution" /> is **within 3 DoFs**, it's probably a **2D (planar) mechanism**.
> If it involves **more than 3 DoFs**, it's almost certainly a **3D (spatial) mechanism**.
:::

### Systematic Joint Analysis Process

<Steps>
1. **Identify all links** in the mechanism (include ground as link 1)

2. **Classify each joint type** and determine constraints imposed

3. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation** to calculate total degrees of freedom

4. **Verify mobility** through physical reasoning and constraint analysis

5. **Check for special cases** (redundant constraints, passive joints)
</Steps>

### Series vs. Parallel Joint Configurations

Understanding how joints combine is critical for designing mechanisms:

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Series Configuration">

    **Joints in series ADD their degrees of freedom:**

    ```
    R + R + R = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 DoF
    R + P + R = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 DoF
    S + R + R = 3 + 1 + 1 = 5 DoF
    ```

    **Design principle:** Use series for cumulative motion and larger workspaces

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Parallel Configuration">

    **Joints in parallel CREATE constraints:**

    Example - Stewart Platform:
    - 6 parallel chains each constrain the platform
    - Result: Controlled 6-DoF motion
    - High stiffness and precision

    **Design principle:** Use parallel for precision and stiffness

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Closure Constraints">

    **When mechanisms form closed loops:**

    - Shared workpieces create **closure constraints**
    - Each rigid connection removes 6 DoF
    - Total system DoF ‚â† sum of individual components

    **Formula for shared workpiece:**
    $$F_{shared} = F_1 + F_2 - 6$$

    **Design principle:** Account for coupling when multiple mechanisms interact

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Special Considerations

:::caution[Common Analysis Pitfalls]
1. **Overconstrained systems** may have F < 0 but still be mobile (passive joints compensate)
2. **Passive joints** don't contribute to actuator count
3. **Redundant constraints** require special analysis methods
4. **Instantaneous DoF** may differ from overall mechanism DoF
5. **Singularities** can temporarily change effective DoF
6. **Closure constraints** in multi-robot systems reduce effective DoF
:::

### Step 1: Calculate Robot 1 DoF (6R Configuration)

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal Robot 1 DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Count links and joints:**

   - Links: n = 7 (6 moving links + ground)
   - Joints: j = 6 (all revolute)
   - Constraints per revolute joint: c = 5

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(7-1) - 6(5)$$

   $$F = 6(6) - 30 = 36 - 30 = 6 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Motion capability:**

   - **Translations:** 3 (X, Y, Z)
   - **Rotations:** 3 (roll, pitch, yaw)
   - **Total:** Full spatial positioning ‚úÖ

4. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 6 (one per revolute joint) ‚úÖ

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Calculate Robot 2 DoF (SCARA: 3R + 1P)

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal SCARA DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Count links and joints:**

   - Links: n = 5 (4 moving links + ground)
   - Joints: j = 4 (3 revolute + 1 prismatic vertical)
   - Total constraints: 3(5) + 1(5) = 20

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(5-1) - [3(5) + 1(5)]$$

   $$F = 6(4) - 20 = 24 - 20 = 4 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **SCARA motion breakdown:**

   - **Horizontal plane:** 2 translational DoF (X, Y)
   - **Vertical:** 1 translational DoF (Z)
   - **Rotation:** 1 about vertical axis (Œ∏<sub>z</sub>)

   **Constraint:** Tool axis remains vertical (no tilt) ‚úÖ

4. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 4 (3 rotary + 1 vertical linear) ‚úÖ

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 3: Calculate Workpiece Positioner DoF (2U)

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal positioner DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Count links and joints:**

   - Links: n = 3 (2 moving segments + ground)
   - Joints: j = 2 (universal joints)
   - Constraints per universal joint: c<sub>U</sub> = 4

   **Reminder:** Each universal joint allows 2 rotational DoF.

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(3-1) - 2(4)$$

   $$F = 6(2) - 8 = 12 - 8 = 4 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Rotation capability:**

   - **First universal joint:** 2 rotational DoF
   - **Second universal joint:** 2 rotational DoF
   - **Total:** 4 rotational DoF for workpiece orientation ‚úÖ

   **Motors required:** 4 (2 per universal joint assembly) ‚úÖ

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 4: Total System Analysis

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal complete system calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Summary table:**

   | Subsystem | Config | Links (n) | Joints (j) | Constraints (Œ£c) | DoF | Actuators |
   |-----------|--------|-----------|------------|------------------|-----|-----------|
   | Robot 1 (6-Axis Welder) | 6R | 7 | 6 | 30 | 6 | 6 |
   | Robot 2 (SCARA) | 3R+1P | 5 | 4 | 20 | 4 | 4 |
   | Workpiece Positioner | 2U | 3 | 2 | 8 | 4 | 4 |
   | **TOTALS** | - | 15 | 12 | 58 | **14** | **14** |

   ‚úÖ

2. **Verification using individual calculations:**

   $$F_{total} = F_{R1} + F_{R2} + F_{pos}$$

   $$F_{total} = 6 + 4 + 4 = 14 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

   **Note:** This assumes **independent operation** (robots not sharing workpiece).

3. **Total actuator requirement:**

   **Independent operation:** 14 motors ‚úÖ

   **Control system needs:**
   - 14 servo motor controllers
   - Real-time coordination computer
   - Synchronized motion planning software

4. **Redundancy analysis:**

   **Robot 1 (6 DoF):**
   - Task requires: 6 DoF (full welding orientation)
   - Available: 6 DoF
   - **Redundancy:** None (minimal configuration) ‚úÖ

   **Robot 2 (4 DoF SCARA):**
   - Task requires: 4 DoF (workpiece positioning)
   - Available: 4 DoF
   - **Redundancy:** None for full 4-DoF tasks ‚úÖ

5. **Shared workpiece constraint analysis:**

   When Robot 1 grips the workpiece held by the positioner:

   **Closure constraints added:** 6 (rigid connection removes 6 DoF)

   **Modified DoF calculation:**

   $$F_{shared} = F_{R1} + F_{pos} - 6$$

   $$F_{shared} = 6 + 4 - 6 = 4 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

   **Interpretation:** The combined system (Robot 1 + Positioner + Workpiece) has only 4 controllable DoF when rigidly connected.

   **Practical meaning:**
   - Positioner must coordinate with Robot 1
   - Cannot move all 10 actuators independently
   - 6 actuators become dependent on the other 4

6. **Critical design insights:**

   **Maximum motors needed:** 14 (independent operation) ‚úÖ

   **Effective DoF when coordinated:**
   - Robot 1 + Positioner + Workpiece: 4 DoF
   - Robot 2 (SCARA): 4 DoF
   - **Total coordinated:** 8 DoF (not 14)

   **Singularity concerns:**
   - Robot 1: Wrist singularities (3 axes align), shoulder singularity
   - Robot 2 (SCARA): Elbow singularity (links collinear)
   - Positioner: Gimbal lock possible ‚úÖ

</Steps>

:::note[Engineering Insight]
This welding cell requires **14 independent actuators** for full mobility, but the **effective DoF** when Robot 1 shares the workpiece with the positioner is reduced to **8 DoF** due to **closure constraints**.

**Key Concept:** The closure constraint formula $F_{shared} = F_1 + F_2 - 6$ shows that rigidly connecting two mechanisms removes 6 DoF (the 3 translations + 3 rotations that would allow independent motion). This is critical for coordinated multi-robot systems.

**Practical Impact:** While the cell has 14 motors, only **8 can move independently** during welding operations - the other 6 are constrained by the rigid gripper connection between Robot 1 and the workpiece.
:::

</details>

  - **Option A:** Spherical wrist (1S) + shaft rotation (1R) + insertion (1P)
  - **Option B:** Universal wrist (1U) + 2 revolute (2R) + insertion (1P)
  - **Option C:** Three revolute wrist (3R) + shaft rotation (1R) + insertion (1P)
</Card>

### Step 1: Analyze Option A - Spherical Wrist Configuration

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal Option A DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **System configuration:**

   - **Proximal (outside patient):** Insertion depth (1P) + shaft rotation (1R)
   - **Distal (inside patient):** Spherical wrist joint (1S at tool tip)
   - Links: n = 4 (insertion shaft + rotating shaft + wrist housing + end-effector + ground)
   - Joints: 1 prismatic + 1 revolute + 1 spherical

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(4-1) - [1(5) + 1(5) + 1(3)]$$

   $$F = 18 - [5 + 5 + 3] = 18 - 13 = 5 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Motion capability breakdown:**

   - **Insertion (P):** 1 translational DoF (depth control)
   - **Shaft rotation (R):** 1 rotational DoF (about insertion axis)
   - **Spherical wrist (S):** 3 rotational DoF (pitch, yaw, roll at tip)
   - **Total:** 2 translation + 3 rotation = 5 DoF ‚úÖ

   **Missing:** 1 translational DoF (cannot move laterally inside patient without pivoting about trocar)

4. **Trocar constraint analysis:**

   **Remote Center of Motion (RCM) constraint:**
   - Tool shaft MUST pivot through trocar point
   - This removes 2 translational DoF (X, Y perpendicular to shaft)
   - Effective internal DoF: 5 - 2 = **3 DoF usable inside patient** ‚úÖ

   **Note:** Lateral motion achieved by pivoting entire instrument through trocar, not internal joints.

5. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 5 (1 linear for insertion + 4 rotary: 1 shaft + 3 in spherical joint) ‚úÖ

6. **Advantages/Disadvantages:**

   **Advantages:**
   - Compact spherical wrist design
   - All 3 rotational DoF at single point (simplified kinematics)
   - Minimum instrument diameter

   **Disadvantages:**
   - Spherical joint mechanically complex
   - Difficult to seal for sterilization
   - Higher friction in compact design

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Analyze Option B - Universal Wrist Configuration

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal Option B DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **System configuration:**

   - **Proximal:** Insertion (1P) + 2 revolute joints (2R for pitch/yaw angles)
   - **Distal:** Universal joint wrist (1U at tool tip)
   - Links: n = 5 (insertion + link 1 + link 2 + wrist + end-effector + ground)
   - Joints: 1P + 2R + 1U

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(5-1) - [1(5) + 2(5) + 1(4)]$$

   $$F = 24 - [5 + 10 + 4] = 24 - 19 = 5 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

   **Note:** Universal joint has 4 constraints (allows 2 rotational DoF).

3. **Motion capability breakdown:**

   - **Insertion (P):** 1 translational DoF
   - **Proximal revolutes (2R):** 2 rotational DoF (instrument pitch/yaw)
   - **Universal wrist (U):** 2 rotational DoF (end-effector orientation)
   - **Total:** 1 translation + 4 rotation = 5 DoF ‚úÖ

4. **Trocar constraint effect:**

   Same RCM constraint as Option A:
   - Effective internal DoF: **3 DoF** (1 insertion + 2 wrist rotations) ‚úÖ
   - Proximal 2R joints used for positioning through trocar pivot

5. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 5 (1 linear + 4 rotary) ‚úÖ

6. **Advantages/Disadvantages:**

   **Advantages:**
   - Universal joint mechanically simpler than spherical
   - Easier to seal and sterilize
   - Better force transmission

   **Disadvantages:**
   - Slightly larger diameter than spherical design
   - 4 rotational DoF instead of 3 (one redundant)

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 3: Analyze Option C - Three Revolute Wrist Configuration

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal Option C DoF calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **System configuration:**

   - **Proximal:** Insertion (1P) + shaft rotation (1R)
   - **Distal:** Three revolute wrist (3R - pitch, yaw, roll)
   - Links: n = 6 (insertion + shaft + wrist link 1 + link 2 + link 3 + end-effector + ground)
   - Joints: 1P + 4R total

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(6-1) - [1(5) + 4(5)]$$

   $$F = 30 - [5 + 20] = 30 - 25 = 5 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Motion capability breakdown:**

   - **Insertion (P):** 1 translational DoF
   - **Shaft rotation (R):** 1 rotational DoF
   - **Wrist (3R):** 3 rotational DoF (Euler angles: pitch-yaw-roll)
   - **Total:** 1 translation + 4 rotation = 5 DoF ‚úÖ

4. **Trocar constraint effect:**

   Effective internal DoF: **3 DoF** (1 insertion + 2 wrist orientations, shaft rotation external) ‚úÖ

5. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 5 (1 linear + 4 rotary) ‚úÖ

6. **Advantages/Disadvantages:**

   **Advantages:**
   - All revolute joints (simplest mechanism)
   - Proven reliability in industrial robots
   - Easy maintenance and sterilization

   **Disadvantages:**
   - Largest diameter (three separate revolute joints)
   - Wrist singularity when axes align
   - Longer distal segment

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 4: Design Comparison and Selection

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal comparison analysis**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Comparative summary table:**

   | Design | Config | DoF | Actuators | Diameter | Wrist Complexity | Force Trans. | Singularities |
   |--------|--------|-----|-----------|----------|------------------|--------------|---------------|
   | Option A | 1P+1R+1S | 5 | 5 | 8 mm | High | Medium | Few |
   | Option B | 1P+2R+1U | 5 | 5 | 9 mm | Medium | High | Medium |
   | Option C | 1P+4R | 5 | 5 | 10 mm | Low | High | Many |

   ‚úÖ

2. **Workspace analysis:**

   All three options provide equivalent **effective DoF = 5** (before trocar constraint).

   After RCM constraint:
   - Insertion depth: 0-200 mm
   - Wrist rotation: ¬±90¬∞ pitch, ¬±90¬∞ yaw
   - Workspace volume: ~150 mm diameter sphere ‚úÖ

3. **Force transmission comparison:**

   **Option A (Spherical):**
   - Force through compact ball joint
   - Mechanical advantage: ~0.7 (friction losses)
   - Achievable grip force: 20N √ó 0.7 = 14N ‚ö†Ô∏è (below requirement)

   **Option B (Universal):**
   - Force through two perpendicular axes
   - Mechanical advantage: ~0.85
   - Achievable grip force: 20N √ó 0.85 = 17N ‚úÖ (meets requirement)

   **Option C (3R Wrist):**
   - Force through series of revolute joints
   - Mechanical advantage: ~0.9
   - Achievable grip force: 20N √ó 0.9 = 18N ‚úÖ (exceeds requirement)

4. **Sterilization and reliability:**

   - **Option A:** Difficult to seal spherical joint ‚Üí infection risk ‚ö†Ô∏è
   - **Option B:** Universal joint can be sealed ‚Üí good ‚úÖ
   - **Option C:** All revolute joints easily sealed ‚Üí excellent ‚úÖ

5. **Final recommendation:**

   **Selected: Option B (1P + 2R + 1U)**

   **Justification:**
   - Meets all DoF requirements (5 total, 3 effective inside patient) ‚úÖ
   - Acceptable diameter (9mm < 10mm trocar) ‚úÖ
   - Good force transmission (17N grip force) ‚úÖ
   - Medium complexity (balance of performance and reliability) ‚úÖ
   - Superior sealing for sterilization ‚úÖ

   **Trade-off:** Slightly larger than Option A, but significantly better force transmission and easier to sterilize than spherical joint. Simpler than Option C with fewer singularities.

6. **Actuator implementation:**

   **5 motors total:**
   - 1 linear actuator (insertion, external)
   - 2 rotary motors (pitch/yaw, external for trocar pivoting)
   - 2 rotary motors (universal wrist, cable-driven to distal end)

   **Cable transmission ratio:** 3:1 to achieve 20N output force ‚úÖ

</Steps>

:::note[Engineering Insight]
The **trocar port creates a Remote Center of Motion (RCM) constraint** that removes 2 translational DoF, reducing the effective internal workspace DoF from 5 to 3. This is a critical example of **external constraints** affecting mechanism mobility.

**Design Principle:** In constrained environments (surgical ports, confined spaces), the **effective DoF** differs from the **calculated DoF**. Engineers must account for workspace constraints, not just kinematic DoF, when selecting joint configurations.

**Surgical Robot Specifics:** All three options have 5 kinematic DoF, but the universal joint (Option B) provides the best **balance of compactness, force transmission, and sterility** - demonstrating that DoF analysis alone is insufficient for real-world design decisions.
:::

</details>

### Step 1: Calculate Individual Finger DoF

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal single finger DoF analysis**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Single finger configuration:**

   - Links: n = 4 (proximal + medial + distal phalanges + palm/ground)
   - Joints: j = 3 (all revolute: Œ∏‚ÇÅ, Œ∏‚ÇÇ, Œ∏‚ÇÉ)
   - Each revolute joint: c = 5 constraints

2. **Apply Gr√ºbler's equation (spatial):**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum_{i=1}^{j} c_i$$

   $$F = 6(4-1) - 3(5)$$

   $$F = 18 - 15 = 3 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

   **Note:** This assumes **3 independent actuators** per finger (not our case).

3. **Motion capability:**

   - Joint 1 (proximal): Rotation Œ∏‚ÇÅ
   - Joint 2 (medial): Rotation Œ∏‚ÇÇ
   - Joint 3 (distal): Rotation Œ∏‚ÇÉ
   - **Total:** 3 rotational DoF per finger ‚úÖ

4. **Underactuation constraint:**

   **With tendon coupling:**
   - All 3 joints driven by **single tendon**
   - Joints move sequentially (not independently)
   - **Effective actuated DoF per finger:** 1 ‚úÖ

   **Passive joints:** Joints 2 and 3 become passive after contact with object.

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Calculate Complete 4-Finger Gripper DoF

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal complete gripper DoF analysis**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Total system without coupling:**

   - Fingers: 4
   - Joints per finger: 3
   - Total joints: 4 √ó 3 = 12 revolute joints
   - Total links: n = 4 fingers √ó 3 phalanges + 1 palm = 13 links

   **If all joints independent:**

   $$F = 6(13-1) - 12(5) = 72 - 60 = 12 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

2. **With tendon coupling constraints:**

   **Coupling mechanism:**
   - Single motor drives 1 main tendon
   - Differential pulley distributes force to 4 finger tendons
   - Each finger tendon drives 3 joints in sequence

   **Additional constraints from coupling:**
   - 4 fingers must close synchronously: 3 coupling constraints
   - 3 joints per finger coupled by single tendon: 2 constraints √ó 4 = 8 constraints
   - **Total coupling constraints:** 3 + 8 = 11 ‚úÖ

3. **Modified Gr√ºbler's equation with coupling:**

   $$F = 6(n-1) - \sum c_i - C_{coupling}$$

   $$F = 72 - 60 - 11 = 1 \text{ DoF}$$ ‚úÖ

   **Result:** The entire 12-joint gripper has **only 1 actuated DoF** (the motor input).

4. **Passive DoF during grasping:**

   When fingers contact fruit:
   - Proximal joints stop when tendon tension balanced
   - Medial and distal joints continue closing (passive motion)
   - **Self-adaptive shape conformance** ‚úÖ

5. **Actuator count:**

   **Motors required:** 1 (single motor for entire gripper) ‚úÖ

   **Mechanical advantage through pulleys:**
   - Motor torque: 0.5 N¬∑m
   - Pulley ratio: 4:1
   - Tendon force: (0.5 N¬∑m) / (0.02 m radius) √ó 4 = 100 N
   - Force per finger: 100 N / 4 = 25 N
   - Fingertip force: 25 N / 5 (mechanical advantage) = 5 N ‚úÖ (meets requirement)

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 3: Size Adaptability Analysis

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal adaptability calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Geometric parameters:**

   - Proximal phalange length: L‚ÇÅ = 40 mm
   - Medial phalange length: L‚ÇÇ = 30 mm
   - Distal phalange length: L‚ÇÉ = 25 mm
   - Maximum finger span (fully open): 120 mm diameter ‚úÖ
   - Minimum finger span (fully closed): 35 mm diameter

2. **Joint angle ranges:**

   - Œ∏‚ÇÅ (proximal): 0¬∞ to 90¬∞
   - Œ∏‚ÇÇ (medial): 0¬∞ to 110¬∞
   - Œ∏‚ÇÉ (distal): 0¬∞ to 120¬∞

3. **Grasping small fruit (40mm tomatoes):**

   **Contact sequence:**
   - All 4 fingers close simultaneously
   - Proximal joints rotate to Œ∏‚ÇÅ ‚âà 65¬∞
   - First contact at **proximal phalanges**
   - Tendon tension stops proximal joints
   - Medial and distal joints continue passively
   - Final configuration: Œ∏‚ÇÅ=65¬∞, Œ∏‚ÇÇ=25¬∞, Œ∏‚ÇÉ=10¬∞ ‚úÖ

   **Grip diameter:** 40 mm ‚úÖ (matches fruit size)

4. **Grasping large fruit (120mm tomatoes):**

   **Contact sequence:**
   - Fingers open to maximum span
   - Proximal joints rotate to Œ∏‚ÇÅ ‚âà 15¬∞
   - First contact at **distal fingertips**
   - Tendon tension distributed across all joints
   - Final configuration: Œ∏‚ÇÅ=15¬∞, Œ∏‚ÇÇ=5¬∞, Œ∏‚ÇÉ=5¬∞ ‚úÖ

   **Grip diameter:** 120 mm ‚úÖ (matches fruit size)

5. **Force distribution verification:**

   **For 40mm fruit (proximal contact):**
   - Contact points: 4 (one per finger)
   - Force per contact: 5N / 4 = 1.25 N
   - **Status:** ‚úÖ Within 2-5N requirement (no bruising)

   **For 120mm fruit (distal contact):**
   - Contact points: 4 (fingertips)
   - Moment arm disadvantage: √ó1.5
   - Effective force: 5N / 1.5 = 3.3 N per contact
   - **Status:** ‚úÖ Within 2-5N requirement

6. **Workspace envelope:**

   **Achievable grip range:** 35-120 mm ‚úÖ
   **Required range:** 40-120 mm ‚úÖ
   **Margin:** 5 mm (acceptable) ‚úÖ

</Steps>

:::note[Engineering Insight]
This underactuated gripper demonstrates how **coupling constraints** can reduce a 12-DoF mechanism to **1 actuated DoF** while maintaining **passive adaptability**. The key insight:

$$F_{actuated} = F_{total} - C_{coupling} = 12 - 11 = 1 \text{ DoF}$$

**Underactuation Principle:** By intentionally **removing independent actuation**, the gripper gains **passive compliance** - allowing automatic shape adaptation to varying fruit sizes without sensors or complex control. This is a powerful example of **constraint-based design** where limiting DoF actually **improves functionality**.

**Practical Advantage:** 1 motor instead of 12 ‚Üí 92% cost reduction + simpler control + inherent robustness.
:::
